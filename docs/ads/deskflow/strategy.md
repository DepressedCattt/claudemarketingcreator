# Deskflow — Creative Strategy

## Campaign Objective
Drive trial signups from support team leads at growing SaaS and e-commerce companies who are drowning in a shared Gmail inbox and losing customers because tickets fall through the cracks.

## Audience Summary
- **Who:** Support team leads and ops managers at companies with 5–50 person teams, currently running support from Gmail or a clunky legacy tool
- **Stage:** Consideration — they know they need a helpdesk, but haven't picked one yet. Zendesk feels too enterprise, Intercom too expensive.
- **Emotional state:** Embarrassed. They know their support is sloppy. Customers complain. Things get missed. They feel like they're duct-taping a system together.
- **What they want:** To look like a company that has its act together — fast response times, nothing slipping through, customers saying "wow, that was quick"

## Key Pain Points (ranked)
1. Tickets vanish into a shared inbox — no ownership, no tracking, no accountability
2. Customers wait hours (or days) and then churn silently — the team only hears about it when a bad review appears
3. No visibility into support quality — no response time data, no satisfaction scores, no way to prove the team is improving

## Desired Emotional Arc
- **Before:** Anxious recognition — "this is us, this is exactly what's happening"
- **During:** Relief and clarity — "oh, this is what it looks like when it works properly"
- **After:** Urgency — "we need this before we lose another customer"

## Marketing Angle
**The Invisible Bleed** — Customers are leaving and you don't even know why.

This angle targets the scariest pain: silent churn. Not the dramatic blowup complaint — the customer who emails once, waits 3 days, gets no reply, and quietly cancels. The business never even knows they lost them. This is more visceral than a feature comparison because it targets the gap between what the team thinks is happening and what's actually happening.

## Hook
> "A customer emailed you 3 days ago. You never replied."

This works because it's a specific, plausible accusation that triggers immediate anxiety. The viewer's first instinct is to check their inbox. It doesn't say "customer support is hard" — it says "you already failed and didn't notice."

## Messaging Pillars
1. **The silent exit** — Customers don't complain. They just leave. You only find out when the revenue dips.
2. **The visibility gap** — You think response times are fine. The data says 14 hours average. You've never measured.
3. **The control shift** — Every ticket owned, every SLA tracked, every customer accounted for. Nothing slips.
4. **The proof** — Real-time dashboards that show exactly how the team is performing — CSAT, first-reply time, resolution rate.

## Headline / Slogan Options
1. "Stop the silent churn." ← RECOMMENDED
2. "Every ticket. Every customer. Every time."
3. "Support that doesn't let people disappear."

## Visual Direction
- **Palette feel:** Deep slate navy with warm amber warning accents — the amber represents the "invisible bleed" (missed tickets, silent churn). Teal/cyan for the solution state.
- **UI density:** Show real product UI — an actual ticket inbox, a dashboard with real metrics, a customer timeline. Not abstract shapes.
- **Mood:** Controlled urgency. Not panicked, not calm. The feeling of realising a problem exists and having the tool to fix it immediately.
- **References:** Linear's dark polish, Stripe's data clarity, Front's inbox design — modern, dense, but readable.

## Platform, Format & Category
- **Primary platform:** YouTube pre-roll, LinkedIn/Facebook paid
- **Format:** 16:9 4K (3840×2160)
- **Duration:** 20 seconds
- **Category:** saas

## Anti-Patterns — What This Ad Must NOT Be
- Not a feature walkthrough — this is about the COST of inaction, not a product tour
- Not cheerful or upbeat — the first half should feel uncomfortable, like catching yourself in a mistake
- No floating orbs or abstract tech visuals — every element is a real piece of support UI
- Not Zendesk energy — no enterprise-speak, no "solution" language. Direct and blunt.
- No stock photography or people illustrations — interface-driven visuals only
